# Reading Room

A lightweight place to collect references, quotes, and synthesis from books, papers, articles, docs, talks, or field notes.

## How to Capture a Source

Keep enough context that the note is useful later without turning it into a full report.

```markdown
## Title

- Source:
- Author:
- Link:
- Date read:
- Why it mattered:

### Takeaways

- Point one.
- Point two.
- Point three.

### Follow-ups

- Question or next read.
```

## Example Entry

### Designing for Serendipity

- Source: Imaginary conference talk
- Author: A thoughtful builder
- Date read: 2026-05-27
- Why it mattered: It framed discovery as something systems can encourage without becoming noisy.

#### Takeaways

- Good tools make forgotten work easy to rediscover.
- A useful archive needs trails, not just storage.
- Search is strongest when paired with curated entry points.

#### Follow-ups

- Add a "related notes" section to each notebook page.
- Try tagging notes by energy level: `seed`, `working`, `evergreen`.

## Synthesis

The best notebook is part library, part workbench. It should preserve finished thinking, but it should also make half-formed thinking feel welcome enough to continue.
